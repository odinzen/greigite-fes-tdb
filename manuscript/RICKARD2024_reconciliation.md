# Rickard, Roberts & Navrotsky (2024) — reconciliation with this Fe–S(–O) assessment

**Source:** Rickard, D., Roberts, A. P., & Navrotsky, A. (2024). *Sedimentary Greigite Formation.*
American Journal of Science 324, 13. https://doi.org/10.2475/001c.121855
(local: `Downloads/121855-sedimentary-greigite-formation.pdf`, text layer extracted)

## What Rickard 2024 claims (with locations)
1. **Greigite is a STABLE sedimentary Fe–S phase, not metastable.** Revised ΔfG°(Fe₃S₄) = **−433.5 kJ/mol**
   (Fig. 1, adopting Shumway 2022), vs older metastable estimates −290.4 (Berner 1967), −308.3 (Rickard & Luther 2007),
   −309.19 (Lemire 2020). Abstract + §1.
2. **Greigite is stable relative to pyrrhotite (FeS₁.₀₉₂) AND smythite.** Large stability area in pe–pH space; dominates
   low-pe / methanic regimes; small zone near the sulfate–sulfide boundary. Abstract, §3.3, §3.6.
3. **Greigite–pyrite are DECOUPLED in formation.** Abstract: "Most sedimentary and biological greigite forms via
   equilibrium reactions involving mackinawite-like precursors, **with no direct coupling of greigite with pyrite**;
   these minerals form independently during sedimentary diagenesis."
4. **The greigite–pyrite *reactions* are equilibrium bookkeeping, not mechanism** (§3.4, verbatim): "These reactions are
   chemical bookkeeping exercises and have no relationship with actual mechanisms… greigite does not necessarily form
   before pyrite; pyrite can be replaced by greigite if conditions facilitating pyrite dissolution occur."
5. **Oxidation sensitivity** governs greigite preservation (protection by low permeability / enclosure / cell membranes).
6. **Stated uncertainty** on greigite free energy = **±0.627 kJ/mol** (aqueous-solubility RMSE propagation); pyrite
   ΔfG° = −160.2 ± 0.1 kJ/mol; mackinawite FeS −98.2 ± 2.4 (Rickard 2006). §2–§3, Table 3.

## How it reconciles with our work
- **Headline agreement (convergent):** Rickard's ΔfG° = −433.5 kJ/mol and our ΔfH° = −432.3 kJ/mol (Subramani 2020, ×3)
  both put greigite on the stable side. Our engine result — greigite preempts pyrrhotite at the measured enthalpy — is the
  same conclusion Rickard reaches independently (greigite stable vs pyrrhotite & smythite). **Rickard corroborates the
  central result; it does not overturn it.**
- **The "decoupling" is a FORMATION-pathway (kinetic/diagenetic) claim, not an equilibrium one.** Equilibrium phase-field
  topology (our Fig. 2 / Fig. 3) is set by Gibbs energies alone and is independent of formation pathway. Rickard *explicitly*
  frames the greigite–pyrite relation as equilibrium "bookkeeping" divorced from mechanism (§3.4) — which is exactly what
  our predominance diagrams are. So there is **no conflict**: our diagrams are the equilibrium statement Rickard endorses.
- **Our decomposition reaction is the THERMAL regime, not the sedimentary one.** The greigite → pyrite + pyrrhotite
  decomposition we quantify (+19.5 ± 7.9 kJ, +2.47σ) is the *closed-system, heating* path — supported directly by
  the DSC/XRD data (greigite→pyrite at 317 °C, →pyrrhotite-3T by 600–700 °C). Rickard's decoupling concerns *low-T
  aqueous diagenesis*. Different regimes; both valid. The report must label which regime each diagram addresses.
- **Oxidation sensitivity triangulates.** Rickard's preservation-by-oxidation-protection ↔ our Fe–S–O result (greigite
  borders magnetite) ↔ the DSC surface-oxidation exotherms (nano → magnetite). Three independent lines agree.
- **Uncertainty divergence — keep the conservative band.** Rickard's ±0.627 kJ/mol is an internal RMSE of an aqueous-
  solubility fit, NOT a full systematic uncertainty; Subramani's ±7.3 kJ/mol-FeS₁.₃₃ is drop-solution calorimetry. We
  retain the **calorimetric ±7.3 (±21.9/Fe₃S₄)** for the on-figure error envelope (honest, conservative) and cite
  Rickard's tighter value as method-specific. Do NOT adopt ±0.627 as the headline.
